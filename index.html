A Hacker's Guide to NYC

Inspired by A Hacker's guide to the Bay Area, this guide is intended for "hackers" interested in the tech scene in NYC. I write from the perspective of a 20-something female front-end web developer who enjoys meeting people at tech meetups, blogs about learning how to code, and enjoys recording music and thinking about civic hacks.

Tech Meetups in NYC This Week
Background

I started frequenting tech meetups -- mostly via meetup.com -- in 2013. I had just transitioned into a full-time front-end web development role at a progressive non-profit, had gone to my first hackathon in November 2012, and was thirsting to meet other developers.

To be honest, I think much of my enthusiasm for tech meetups stemmed from the fact that I worked at a completely distributed office -- which meant that everyone worked from home. I loved interacting with my co-workers online, but to be able to talk shop about what technologies people were using or learning, and to share about victories and struggles, was a breath of fresh air away from the lonely intensity of the computer screen.

Silicon "Alley" can't quite compete with Silicon Valley for the tech mecca trophy, but it is growing -- and the tech industry is fast becoming NYC's second largest sector. The city is big enough that there are often multiple meetups or hackathons going on at once, but small enough that I've met acquaintances that have become friends.

More than 1000 tech and digital companies proudly show off their "Made in NY" labels, too; you can check out the full list of NYC-based tech companies here.

Where do I meet other developers?

Hackathons

Hackathons are a great way to meet other developers with a wide range of experience. Although whether or not the hacks tend to be complete is questionable, going to a hackathon is a great way to meet new friends, pair program, and learn about unfamiliar frameworks or languages. TechCrunch hosts a hackathon in NYC once a year, and many hackathons have been hosted at the co-working space at Alley NYC. Gary's Guide and Alley NYC are both great weekly mailing lists to be on if you want the latest news about upcoming hackathons in the city.

Hacker Hours and Code Crew

Think: Study Hall. Or a productive networking party, where it's not taboo to be on your phone/laptop, but where people are still open to chatting. I've made the acquaintance of some very experienced and new developers at Hacker Hours and Code Crew -- which conveniently meets at the same location (Alley NYC) on Sunday afternoons from approximately 12-4pm. Code Crew has started organizing some more formal and specific events for those interested in studying a specific programming language or subject, but Hacker Hours has continued to be pretty informal. Bring in your laptop, say hello, ask for help, help others if you can. It's a gift economy.

Language or topic-specific

If you want to get more language or topic-specific, NYC has a host of meetups. The Wordpress meetup hosts a speaker event for $6 (pizza and water bottle included!) every third Tuesday at Alley NYC, and has featured Buddypress developers to former Automattic employees. Ruby on Rails is very popular, and has its own meetups, though I haven't been to any yet. The New York Python Meetup is also very active, and has weekly informal study groups/office hours on Saturdays and Sundays as well as speaker events. For those interested in a more structured learning environment, the Pluralsight meetup (hosted at Microsoft's offices in midtown) features weekly video sessions on anything from git to node.js. Spaces at Pluralsight run out pretty quickly, though -- so make sure you sign up.

Women-oriented

There are quite a few meetups/events for women in tech if you look closely. In September 2013, Facebook and Birchbox co-hosted target="_blank">a fun night of networking and board games for women engineers. For those looking for a hackthon where the gender ratio is more or less 50-50,Hack 'n Jill takes place once or twice a year in the city, and is something to check out. Ladies Who Code hosts monthly pizza-and-cupcake-filled small gathering hack nights, and Write Speak Code, an inaugural professional development conference for women developers by women developers was also held in NYC in June.

What are some developer bootcamps or schools in the city?

Hacker School

A 3-month writer's retreat for programmers. They have a laudable developer contract.

Fullstack Academy

Specializes in Ruby on Rails.

App Academy

A Ruby on Rails shop.

The Flatiron School

The Flatiron School takes students through a 12-week full-time intensive training regime in web development (with a focus on Ruby on Rails).

General Assembly

The meat of General Assembly's courses are 3-4 month long front-end or Ruby on Rails web development courses. They also offer several weekend-long or day-long or hour-long short-term courses. The best way to find out about their course offerings is to sign up for their mailing list.

NYU

NYU's School of Continuing and Professional Studies offers a certificate in web development.

What can I do for fun in NYC?

Between the Metropolitan Museum of Art, MoMA, Central Park, Prospect Park, other interest-based meetups (writing, Harry Potter, Reddit), comedy shows, Broadway, rock shows at Madison Square Park, concerts at Lincoln Center, rock climbing in Brooklyn, free weekly summer movies at Bryant Park... there's a lot more to do than to just go to tech meetups in the city.

I'm not the expert on the best restaurants in NYC, but suffice it to say that they do exist!

Rent (quick guide)

Many jokes and blog posts have been produced about the hazards of finding a good apartment in NYC. In my experience, Craigslist can be helpful, but roommate searching via your network of friends (or anyone you might be acquainted with) is even more helpful. Some people swallow the fees and use brokers to some success.

Transportation (quick guide)

Unless you live more than 15 minutes walking distance from a subway station, trust me -- you won't need a car! The subways can be confusing during the weekend when the local tracks run express or when parts of the subways are shut down, but in general you'll be able to make your destination with a couple of transfers along the way. If you get lost during your first few weeks here, don't be afraid to ask a stranger for directions. The buses in the city are also a convenient way to get around. You can ride on them for the same price as a subway ride. Most people who live in NYC buy monthly subway passes, which allows them to take a subway or a bus as many times as they want.
